News from around the Bay -

From the Georgian Bay Association

“Many Factors Affect Bass Populations

A healthy bass population needs a good habitat for spawning and feeding - including habitat for it's favourite food, the crayfish.  Bass also need warm spring weather to get young fry off to a good start - cool weather reduces the number of fry that survive for another year. The sharp increase in cormorants in the 1990s was one of the factors that resulted in heavy mortality of young bass.

 

But most important...

...sport fishing has put heavy pressure on the bass population -

- more cottagers and tourists, with longer vacations, easier access to the best fishing, and technical

stuff like fish-finders -

- these changes have increased the take and left fewer big bass to continue this important resource.

 

New Regulations

Bass fishing regulations haven't changed for many decades - and - those regulations don't protect the

large bass that produce the greatest number of young bass.  A new approach is needed to regulate the catch.  The objective is clear - to catch more and larger bass - but keep only a few small bass for a breakfast treat.

A recent AP story was detailed the low water concerns of the Great Lakes.  In case your newspaper did not carry the story, here are a few highlights:

 

·        Water levels may hit record lows by the end of the summer;

·        Runoff from melting snow, which accounts for 40% of the lakes’ seasonal replenishment is way down and higher-than-usual temperatures have accelerated evaporation;

·        From an ecological standpoint, the water decline, although unusually steep, is part of the natural cycle that is mostly beneficial because it prevents any plant species from dominating.
